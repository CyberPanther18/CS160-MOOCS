</h3>
<h3><strong><span style="color:rgb(0,147,187); font-size: 22px" trebuchet="" data-redactor-tag="span">Course Description:</span></strong></h3><p class="MsoNormal" style>
	<span style>Those trying to create social change often revisit a familiar question: how do we know what works? This simple question can be alarmingly tough to answer; especially when the change we are targeting is complex and multi causal.  If we're to have any hope of answering such questions it is critical that we collect data about our social impact. Such data allows us to learn and to improve our performance against our social targets over time.  Lacking this reliable and timely data, a social not-for-profit, investor or business may be left hoping rather than knowing that they've improved the lives of their customers.  </span>
</p><p class="MsoNormal" style>
	<span style> </span>
</p><p class="MsoNormal" style>
	<span style>Of course social professionals of all stripes have been working for years to monitor and evaluate the impact of social programs, products and services.  But the timeline for gaining actionable insights to figure out how to achieve the greatest impact has often been too slow, especially for social businesses. If start-ups and tech companies can use principles of agile development to gain customer insights, can social enterprises serving the poor also leverage emerging tools and “lean" design principles to measure impact and shorten learning cycles? Can we build data collection into decision making to improve impact in real time instead of just proving impact years after the fact? </span>
</p><p class="MsoNormal" style>
	<span style> </span>
</p><p class="MsoNormal" style>
	<span style>In the last ten years, the infrastructure that allows us to connect to end customers – even end customers living on just a few dollars a day – has improved dramatically.   The proliferation of mobile phones and a host of technologies both new (like SMS surveying) and old (such as call-centers) make it easier to collect data from the end customers we aim to serve.  Innovative survey questionnaires like the Progress out of Poverty Index developed by the Grameen Foundation allow us to gather robust data without having to ask customers hundreds of questions.  And data collection can be integrated into existing company-customer touch points, whether through mobile phones or an in person sales team, making it considerably less onerous.  </span>
</p><p class="MsoNormal" style>
	<span style> </span>
</p><p class="MsoNormal" style>
	<span style>Acumen's Lean Data Initiative capitalizes on these important developments, putting decision-making tools in the hands of social enterprises that want to gather real-time data directly from customers.  This impact measurement approach is uniquely suited to social investors and the enterprises they support, both of which face the dual pressures of time and cash constraints but still need real data to know that they are delivering on their social, and financial, objectives.  And, even if you're working for neither of these types of organizations and are just interested in uncovering new ways to understand social impact, we believe that the principles and tools that underline the Lean Data approach will help you learn faster and position you to make decisions that maximize your impact to better serve your stakeholders.</span>
</p><p class="MsoNormal" style>
	<span style> </span>
</p><p class="MsoNormal" style>
	<span style>In this 7 hour course from Acumen, you'll learn more about the Lean Data mindset and be introduced to the tools and methods we use. You will scope your own Lean Data Initiative, beginning with mapping the current information flow within your enterprise and pinpointing places where data could be collected in the least disruptive way. Finally, through case study examples from Acumen's portfolio, you will get a feel for the on-the-ground challenges and rewards associated with implementing a Lean Data project.</span>
</p><div>
